## Full post

You trained hard for two weeks. Maybe three.

Then something happened — a busy week at work, a missed session that turned into two, a Tuesday where the routine just didn't hold — and the whole thing stopped.

If you've been through this more than once, the usual explanation is discipline. That you didn't want it enough. That you're someone who starts things and doesn't finish them.

I don't think that's what happened.

---

Here's what the data actually says.

Research on fitness program adherence consistently shows that the highest dropout window is between weeks three and six. Not week one, when motivation is high and the program is still new. Not week eight, when people have already built momentum. Week three.

The reason is specific: week three is where the accountability structure of most programs runs out. The novelty is gone. The initial soreness and progress signals have settled. And there's nothing built into the program for this moment.

No check-in. No coach who notices when you've gone quiet. No adjustment when your schedule changes and the original plan no longer fits. The program does what it was designed to do — give you sessions to follow — but it has no mechanism for when things get hard.

One missed session becomes two. Two becomes a decision that it's easier to stop than to explain why you're starting again.

This is not a discipline problem. The structure ran out before the program did.

---

Six weeks sounds like a specific number, and it is.

Six weeks covers the full dropout window. It's long enough to install a habit past the point where the novelty carries it, and short enough that there's still a clear accountability structure in place when week three hits.

Inside 6FIX, there's a check-in every week — not a form, but a real review. What happened, what the data shows, what needs to change. The training plan adjusts based on that. So does the nutrition. If your schedule changed in week two, the plan in week three reflects that — not what it assumed week two would look like.

The check-in is the structural element that most programs don't have. It's also the thing that catches the moment before it becomes a reason to quit.

By week five, most people in the program report that the habits have stopped feeling like decisions. Not because they became more disciplined. Because the structure held long enough for the habits to install.

That's the only thing 6FIX is trying to do: hold the structure through the window where most programs lose people.
