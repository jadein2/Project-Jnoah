## Blog post structure

Every blog post follows this structure — in this order. Do not rearrange.

### 1. Hook (opening paragraph — 2–4 sentences)
Opens with the problem, not the solution. Not the program name. Not a welcome.

The hook must:
- Name a specific situation the reader recognizes
- Create the feeling: "this is about me"
- Make the reader want the next paragraph

**Strong hook patterns:**
- Open with the problem happening in real time: "You trained hard for three weeks. Then you stopped. And you're not sure why."
- Open with a direct challenge to a common belief: "Consistency isn't about discipline. It's about having a plan when discipline runs out."
- Open with a specific observation: "Most people quit four weeks in. Not because they're lazy — because the structure they were using ran out."

**Weak hooks (avoid):**
- Opening with the blog title reworded
- Opening with "In this blog post, we'll talk about..."
- Opening with a generic fitness statistic
- Opening with the program name or brand name

---

### 2. Science or insight (body — 2–4 short paragraphs)
Explains the root cause of the problem or the mechanism behind the solution. Keeps it practical — not academic.

Rules:
- One clear explanation per paragraph
- Short paragraphs — 2–4 sentences max per paragraph
- Use real-world examples or coaching observations, not textbook language
- If citing research, do it briefly and plainly ("Studies show X" → "Research on muscle adaptation shows that...")
- No jargon unless the audience clearly knows it

This section answers: *Why does this happen? What is actually going on?*

---

### 3. Action (practical steps — 3–5 bullets or short paragraphs)
The specific, practical things the reader can do. Not vague advice. Not general tips.

Rules:
- Each action must be specific enough to do today
- Lead with the verb: "Start with..." · "Pick one..." · "Schedule..." · "Replace..."
- 3–5 actions maximum — do not overwhelm
- Can be formatted as short bullets or short numbered steps
- Each action should stand alone — readable without the ones before it

**Bad:** "Be more consistent with your nutrition."
**Good:** "Pick two meals you eat most weeks and make them your defaults. Stop deciding from scratch every day."

---

### 4. Close (1–2 sentences + CTA)
Wraps the post with one clear statement that anchors the main idea. Then the CTA.

The close is not a summary of everything said. It is the one thing the reader should walk away remembering.

**Examples:**
- "Structure is not a restriction. It's what makes progress possible."
- "Three sessions a week, planned in advance, beats five random sessions every time."

Then CTA — on its own line.

## Word count

**Target:** 400–550 words
**Hard ceiling:** 600 words

Urban Strong blogs are not long-form articles. They are short, practical, coach-voice pieces built for busy adults who scan before they read. If the post is over 550 words, cut — do not pad.

## CTA system

| Context | CTA |
|---|---|
| Urban Strong blog (default) | Book a Free Assessment → urbanstrongfit.com/book-free-assessment |
| Restart Fitness PH blog | Book a Discovery Call → restartfitnessph.com/start |
| Soft CTA (awareness post) | Save this · Share this with someone who needs it |
| Program-specific post | Apply for the 6-Week Reset · Talk to a Coach |

**Rules:**
- One CTA per post
- Place CTA at the end of the close — on its own line
- Do not bury the CTA in the middle of a paragraph
- Do not use fake urgency ("Limited spots available!" unless it's actually true)

## Headline rules

The headline is the most important line in the post — it determines whether anyone reads it.

**Format:** Lead with the outcome or the problem. Not the brand. Not the program.

**Strong patterns:**
- Problem-forward: "Why You Keep Quitting After Four Weeks (And What to Do Instead)"
- Outcome-forward: "How to Train Three Times a Week and Actually See Results"
- Myth-busting: "You Don't Need More Motivation — You Need This"
- Practical guide: "The Simple Eating Rule That Makes Fat Loss Less Complicated"

**Avoid:**
- Clickbait that the post doesn't deliver on
- Generic titles: "Tips for Better Fitness" / "How to Be Healthier"
- Leading with the program name in the headline for cold-traffic posts
- Question headlines that are too vague: "Are You Training the Right Way?"

## SEO guidance (light-touch)

Urban Strong blogs are primarily written for people, not search engines. But a few simple rules apply:

- Pick one primary keyword per post and use it naturally in: the headline · the first paragraph · one subhead (if used) · the CTA area
- Do not force the keyword — if it sounds unnatural, drop it
- Use the keyword as people actually search it, not a jargon version
- Keep meta description in mind: the first 125–150 characters of the post often become the meta description — make the opening count
- Do not stuff keywords. One is enough.

## Formatting rules

- Short paragraphs: 2–4 sentences max
- No wall-of-text sections
- Use subheads only when the post is long enough to need navigation (usually not needed under 500 words)
- Bullets only for the action section — not throughout the post
- No bold for decoration — bold only for the single most important line in a section if needed
- No emoji unless specifically requested

## Voice and tone

**Urban Strong blog voice:** Direct, coach-led, grounded, practical. A coach writing for a smart adult — not a brand writing for a customer. No throat-clearing. No motivational padding at the end.

**Restart Fitness PH blog voice:** Same directness, slightly more editorial. Calm authority. Structured and systems-oriented. More likely to reference The Restart Fitness System by name.

Apply `/GLOBAL/anti-ai-output-rules.md` fully — especially the Anti-AI Writing Protocol.

## Topic sources

Good blog topics come from:
- Questions members ask repeatedly
- Mistakes seen in training or check-ins
- Common objections from prospects
- Patterns in member drop-off or plateaus
- Nutrition struggles that come up in coaching
- Beginner fears before their first session
- Common fitness myths circulating locally

**Do not write blogs for topics that have no real audience demand.** Every blog should answer a question someone actually asked or solve a problem someone actually has.
