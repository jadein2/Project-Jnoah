# Discovery Call Engine

The full framework for running a discovery call — from the opening question to the close decision.

Source: Coach Jap system design session — 2026-05-19

Load this alongside client-onboarding.md. This file covers Stage 3 only.

---

## What this call is

A discovery call is a structured conversation — not a pitch. You are listening for whether this person is a fit for the program. The goal is a clear decision: go or no-go. No vague follow-ups. No "I'll think about it" closes that go nowhere.

Duration: 20–40 minutes. Not a free coaching session.

---

## Call structure (run in order)

```
Trigger → Goal → Background → Root Cause → Commitment
→ Program Presentation → Qualification Check → Close
```

Each section has a primary question and optional sub-probes. Use the sub-probes when the answer is thin or vague. Do not ask all of them every call.

---

## Section 1 — Trigger

**Primary question:**
> "What made you reach out to Restart Fitness PH specifically — what happened?"

You are looking for a specific moment or a buildup of frustration. Vague answers ("I just want to get fit") are a yellow flag. Specific answers ("My doctor flagged my bloodwork," "I couldn't keep up with my kids," "I've tried three gyms and quit all of them") signal real urgency.

**Sub-probes (use when needed):**
- "How long has this been a problem?"
- "What finally made you do something about it now?"
- "Was there a specific moment that pushed you to reach out?"

**What you're listening for:** Urgency. Is there a real reason to act now, or is this a "someday" conversation?

---

## Section 2 — Goal

**Primary question:**
> "What's your goal — what does success look like for you?"

Let them describe it in their words before reflecting it back. Do not name their goal for them.

**Sub-probes (use when needed):**
- "If we worked together for 90 days and it went well, what would be different?"
- "Is this more about how you look, how you feel, or how you perform?"
- "Is there a number, a date, or an event you're working toward?"

**What you're listening for:** Is the goal specific enough to build a program around? Is it something the Restart Fitness System can actually deliver? Goals that are too vague ("I just want to be healthier") need to be sharpened before you can qualify the lead.

---

## Section 3 — Background

**Primary question:**
> "Have you done any structured coaching or training before?"

This is not a test — you are calibrating what level of programming and coaching support they need.

**Sub-probes (use when needed):**
- "What have you tried before and what happened?"
- "Did you work with a coach or do it on your own?"
- "What made you stop?"

**What you're listening for:** Experience level (complete beginner vs. returning client vs. trained but directionless). Red flags: gym-hopper pattern (joined, quit, joined, quit) with no reflection on why. This isn't disqualifying — but it tells you where the real problem is.

---

## Section 4 — Root Cause

**Primary question:**
> "In your honest opinion — what do you think has been getting in the way?"

This question surfaces self-awareness. The best clients can name the problem. The ones who blame everything external (schedule, gym, equipment) with no ownership are harder to coach.

**Sub-probes (use when needed):**
- "Was it consistency? Knowing what to do? Motivation?"
- "Looking back, what would have had to be different for it to have worked?"
- "Is the problem the plan, or following through on it?"

**What you're listening for:** Ownership and coachability. You cannot coach someone who doesn't believe they are part of the equation. Also: is the problem something a coaching program solves, or is it something else (health condition, life circumstance, readiness)?

---

## Section 5 — Commitment

**Primary question:**
> "How much time can you realistically put toward this every week — training, recovery, all of it?"

Get a real number. Then pressure-test it.

**Sub-probes (use when needed):**
- "What does your typical week look like right now?"
- "Are there days that are consistently free or consistently blocked?"
- "If sessions are [X] times a week, does that fit what your schedule actually allows?"

**What you're listening for:** Reality. Someone who says "I can train every day" when they work 60-hour weeks is not telling you the truth — or they don't know what they're signing up for. The goal is to set a program around what they will actually do, not what sounds good on the call.

---

## Program presentation

Only present the program after all five sections. Do not pitch before you've listened.

**Frame it directly:**
> "Based on what you've shared, here's what I think applies to you."

Then describe:
1. Which program (90-Day, 6FIX, or custom) and why it fits their situation
2. What the structure looks like (session frequency, how coaching works, check-in cadence)
3. What the outcome is if they follow it
4. What it costs and what the options are

Keep this under 5 minutes. You are presenting, not persuading. The conversation already built the case — the presentation connects it.

**Do not:**
- List every feature of the program
- Pitch while they are still processing what you said
- Use pricing as an opener ("it's only X")

---

## Qualification check (internal — do not read aloud)

Before closing, run through this internally:

| Check | Pass | Concern |
|---|---|---|
| Goal is specific and achievable | Can name it clearly | Too vague to build around |
| Urgency is real | Specific trigger or deadline | "Someday" framing |
| Coachability signal | Names their own role in the problem | Full external blame |
| Schedule is realistic | Committed time matches program requirements | Mismatch between stated and actual availability |
| Financial commitment | No hesitation or evasion when price was raised | "I need to check" with no follow-up plan |

Three or more concerns = no-go. One or two = coach's call. All pass = go.

---

## Close — Go decision

> "Based on everything you shared, I think this is a strong fit. I'll send you the invoice today — once that's settled we book the onboarding call and get started."

Then stop. Do not over-explain. Let them respond.

If they have questions, answer them. If they hesitate, ask:
> "What's the one thing you want to think through before committing?"

That surfaces the real objection. Address it once. Do not loop.

---

## Close — No-go decision

> "I want to be honest with you — I don't think now is the right time for this program. Here's what I'd suggest instead: [specific alternative or referral]."

Be direct. Be brief. Be respectful.

Do not string someone along because you feel bad saying no. That wastes their time and yours.

---

## Post-call actions

If go:
- Send invoice same day (see client-onboarding.md Stage 4)
- Do not move to pre-onboarding until payment is confirmed

If no-go:
- No invoice sent
- Optional: short follow-up message with the alternative you mentioned
- Log the outcome in the client file (if one was started) or note in your lead tracker
