# 1. Executive Summary

Urban Strong Fitness is a coached fitness business built around structured training, high-value coaching, accountability, and a supportive community. It is not positioned as a basic access-only gym where members simply use equipment on their own. Its strongest business advantage is the coaching system: members are guided, assessed, onboarded, corrected, progressed, and reviewed.

The business has historically offered group classes, personal training, nutrition coaching, workshops, foundational onboarding, and specialized training tracks. The core opportunity is to systematize the business so it can operate with less owner dependence while improving sales, retention, member experience, and profitability.

The main operational goal is to move from a founder-dependent gym into a structured coaching facility with clear roles, clear SOPs, measurable KPIs, and repeatable client journeys.

The current strategic membership target is to move from approximately **44 active members** toward **100 active members**. Earlier historical business targets included growing from around 35-50 members toward 60+ members. This manual uses the 100-member target as the current growth direction.

Urban Strong Fitness should be run as a premium local coaching facility. Its value should not be defended only through equipment, location, or class access. Its value should be defended through coaching standards, onboarding, accountability, progress reviews, community, and measurable outcomes.

# 5. Services and Offers

## 5.1 Group Class Membership

The group class is the main coached training product. It should focus on strength, conditioning, movement quality, health, fat loss, consistency, and general physical preparedness.

The class should not be marketed primarily as hardcore CrossFit to beginners. It should be presented as a coached training system for people who want to get stronger, fitter, and more consistent.

## 5.2 Personal Training

Personal training is the premium service. It is for clients who need more individual attention, flexible scheduling, custom programming, or faster progress.

Personal training should include:

- Goal consultation
- Movement assessment
- Personalized program
- Session booking in advance
- Coach-led execution
- Progress review
- Nutrition and habit guidance when included

## 5.3 Semi-Private / Small Group Training

Small group training is designed for 2-4 people. Each client can follow an individualized or adjusted program while sharing the coaching session.

This is useful for:

- Friends or spouses training together
- People who want personal attention at lower cost than 1-on-1
- Clients who need more structure than group class
- Off-peak schedule utilization

## 5.4 On-Ramp / Foundations Program

The On-Ramp program is mandatory or strongly recommended for beginners. It ensures members learn basic movement patterns before entering regular classes.

Core movement priorities:

- Hinge
- Squat
- Press
- Pull
- Lunge
- Core control
- Basic conditioning mechanics

The On-Ramp protects the client and the business by improving safety, movement quality, and onboarding experience.

## 5.5 Nutrition Coaching

Nutrition coaching supports weight loss, muscle gain, health improvement, and performance. It should be delivered through clear and simple systems, not overwhelming diet rules.

Key structure:

- Intake form
- Body composition baseline
- Goal setting
- Meal plan or meal framework
- Protein target
- Food quality education
- Habit tracking
- Weekly or bi-weekly check-ins
- Progress adjustments

## 5.6 Workshops

Workshop topics may include:

- Beginner nutrition
- Fat loss basics
- Meal prep
- Mobility
- Strength foundations
- Weightlifting technique
- Hybrid conditioning
- Health and habits

Workshops can be used for member retention, lead generation, and community engagement.

## 5.7 Hybrid / Performance Training

This training track is for individuals with existing fitness background who want more performance-based training. It may include running, rowing, skiing, biking, sled push, carries, wall balls, burpees, and strength work.

Use careful language when referencing HYROX-style training. If not officially affiliated, describe it as:

- Hybrid fitness training
- Race-style conditioning
- Functional endurance training
- HYROX-style preparation, if allowed in context

## 5.8 Booty Class

A special class concept led by M using a stepper. This should be treated as a specialty class or campaign-specific offer, not necessarily the main business identity.

## 5.9 Corporate Wellness

Corporate wellness can be offered to local companies. Possible inclusions:

- Group access
- Scheduled coaching
- Assessment days
- Attendance reports
- Health education sessions
- Corporate pricing tiers

Corporate clients may require official receipts, agreements, and proper documentation.

# 12. Coach Operations and Standards

## 12.1 Coach Role

A coach is responsible for safety, education, movement quality, energy, and member progress. A coach is not just a timer or workout announcer.

## 12.2 Coaching Standards

Every coach must:

- Arrive early
- Review the program before class
- Prepare equipment
- Brief the class clearly
- Start coaching during the warm-up
- Demonstrate movements properly
- Scale movements based on ability
- Prioritize ROM before speed and power
- Correct unsafe movement
- Track member performance when needed
- Maintain class control
- Close class professionally

## 12.3 Cueing Order

Coaches should use this cueing hierarchy:

1. Verbal cue
2. Visual cue
3. Tactile cue, only when appropriate and consent-based

This allows the coach to manage large classes efficiently while protecting client comfort and professionalism.

## 12.4 Coaching Principles

Urban Strong Fitness coaching principles:

- Start coaching immediately after the whiteboard brief.
- Use the warm-up to build movement patterns.
- Use verbal, visual, then tactile cues.
- Evaluate go-to progressions regularly.
- Prioritize range of motion before speed and load.
- Meet clients where they are.
- Build confidence before intensity.

## 12.5 Class Presence

Coach must be visible, active, and attentive. The coach should not stay seated or distracted during class.

## 12.6 Coach Conduct

Coaches must:

- Avoid humiliating members
- Avoid unsafe challenges
- Avoid inappropriate comments
- Avoid favoritism
- Avoid using phones unnecessarily during class
- Respect client privacy
- Maintain professional boundaries
- Report incidents immediately

# 15. Free Assessment Protocol

## 15.1 Purpose

The Free Assessment is used to understand the client, build trust, determine the correct starting point, and recommend the right program.

## 15.2 Assessment Flow

1. Welcome and rapport
2. Goal discussion
3. Training history
4. Health and injury screen
5. Schedule and availability
6. Body composition baseline if applicable
7. Movement screen
8. Program recommendation
9. Pricing discussion
10. Next step and payment

## 15.3 Core Questions

- What is your main goal right now?
- Why is this goal important to you?
- What have you tried before?
- What made you stop or struggle?
- How many times per week can you realistically train?
- Do you have injuries or medical concerns?
- Are you more interested in group class, personal training, or guided access?
- What support do you need most: training, nutrition, accountability, or all of them?

## 15.4 Movement Screen Priorities

At minimum, assess:

- Squat
- Hinge
- Press
- Basic core control
- Basic mobility restrictions

The goal is not to judge the client. The goal is to determine the safest and most appropriate starting point.

## 15.5 Closing Recommendation

The coach should recommend only one clear next step. Too many options create confusion.

Example:

“Based on your goal, training background, and movement screen, your best starting point is the On-Ramp. This gives us time to teach the foundations, adjust movements, and prepare you before joining regular classes.”

# 16. On-Ramp and Foundations SOP

## 16.1 Purpose

The On-Ramp protects beginners from being thrown into regular classes without enough movement preparation. It also improves retention because the client feels guided from the beginning.

## 16.2 Who Needs On-Ramp

Required or strongly recommended for:

- Beginners
- New members with no functional fitness background
- Returning clients after long inactivity
- Clients with movement limitations
- Clients who lack confidence
- Clients joining group classes for the first time

## 16.3 On-Ramp Assessment Requirement

New members must undergo a movement assessment before starting foundational movement exercises. The coach conducting the On-Ramp must be able to identify movement limitations and mechanics issues.

Priority screening:

- Hinge
- Squat
- Press

## 16.4 On-Ramp Pathways

### On-Ramp to Group Class / CrossFit-Style Training

Pathway: Fundamental movement track.

Focus:

- Squat mechanics
- Hinge mechanics
- Press mechanics
- Basic pulling
- Core control
- Safe pacing
- Class readiness

### On-Ramp to Hybrid Track

Requirement: Physical Fitness Test.

The Hybrid track is for individuals with existing fitness background. The PFT should screen readiness for higher conditioning volume.

## 16.5 Hybrid PFT

- Held twice per month
- Free of charge
- Used to qualify people for Hybrid track
- Needs dedicated ad/campaign
- Targets people with existing fitness background

## 16.6 Ascension Day

After On-Ramp completion, hold an “Ascension Day.”

Purpose:

- Celebrate completion
- Confirm readiness
- Introduce member to regular class
- Create a positive milestone
- Improve retention

## 16.7 Scheduling Rule

Create a calendar schedule for coaches to avoid double booking On-Ramp, personal training, and group classes.
